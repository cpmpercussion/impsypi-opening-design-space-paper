The paper investigates the design space of AI-enabled hardware musical performance instruments via a 2-year longitudinal autobiographical artistic study of using their custom AI-enabled hardware musical instrument platform. The platform allows anyone to train an AI model (in this case, a MDRNN) on their own small data on a regular computer, and use it to control the parameters of the hardware musical instrument of their choice via MIDI and OSC communication. The AI model runs on an compact affordable Raspberry Pi Zero 2 W customized by the author to enable easy setup and connectivity to work with any compatible hardware musical instrument for interactive music performance.

A total of 16 performances and demonstrations were done. Tests were conducted using a Korg Volca FM, Arturia MicroFreak and Roland S-1 hardware synthesizers. The human and AI share the control (via turn-taking with human priority) over the synthesizers. Design findings around small data model crafting, fast human-AI input interleaving and inclusive prototyping are well highlighted.

Overall, the paper and work is great. The strengths and weaknesses of the platform are explored along with design implications of the work on human-AI interaction in music performance. The author provides enough details for reproducibility (especially for the target audience / users) and the general sections in the paper make sense. #### Overview

This work documents and reflects on the development of an intelligent-system performance tool using an artist-centred methodology. The work introduces the system, which is lightweight and can run on accessible hardware, providing details on its performance. The author also discusses their personal experience working with and evolving the tool, sharing design insights such as the value of remapping and the importance of designing tools that can be used by non-AI experts.

#### Originality

This work is original in that it documents a long-term (2 year) development of a an AI performance tool within an artistic practice. It presents novel insights on small custom data training, utilising limited resources for AI performance and introducing workarounds such as remapping to give more control to the artist.

#### Rigor

This work would benefit from better reporting of the author’s experience with the different prototypes. Directly recounting specific examples, perhaps as ethnographic vignettes, would better situate the design implications. I would recommend the following: After first introducing a prototype, briefly outline a particular experience you had with it which is relevant to findings and briefly analyse it. (I know this is somewhat done, but I want to emphasis a clear shred structure, as right now it is difficult to directly map these prototypes to examples).

More is needed to outline your methodology. I acknowledge this is a reflection but understanding how you went about evolving this work is important. Work is akin to practice-based methods and it would be worth mentioning this and providing an overview of the methodology for the reviewer.

#### Significance

This paper offers some interesting insights, particularly around fast remapping and use of small datasets. Although other insights are less significant (5.3, 5.4).

#### Presentation

The work is generally presented well, but there are minor errors outlined in the minor comments section.

#### Strengths

- The software is well documented, including information on benchmarking and speed testing, providing the reader a good insight into its design.
- The artistic-centred approach situates this work in real practice.
- The system is designed to be lightweight and accessible for non-AI experts.

#### Weaknesses

- This paper would benefit from a better description of the methodology, providing details on the various approaches employed by the artist. This should be presented as its own section before outlining the system development and should be compared to previous work using artist-centred development.
- The various performances and experiences should be documented better and might benefit from a shared structure between the various experiences. For example, overview of experiment -> specific example of a notable interaction -> link this to the finding. Of course these points are sometimes already covered, but a shared structure would help the reader better understand the design implications discussed later.

#### Decision

I believe this work does progress the field of interactive music performances and inspires a shift in how we think about interactive musical performance with AI. Because of this I would lean my decision to a possible accept. However, The paper is limited by the weaknesses outlined in the weakness section of this review and I would suggest the authors address the below comments. Overall, I commend the author on an interesting paper!

    The paper describes a music AI platform the authors developed, consisting of a Rasberry Pi-based hardware component and an MDRNN-based software component which generates MIDI signals given a preceding MIDI sequence. The platform can be embedded in musical devices to control the devices via MIDI signals. The authors describe and share insights from several case studies of instruments created with this platform (they could be more explicit about enumerating these case studies). They argue for an approach to music AI, embodied by their system, which emphasizes small-data AI models, a focus on mapping rather than training to update model behavior, and input interleaving (by which I think they mean alternating between human and automatic-AI control of generated audio/MIDI signals - they could be more explicit about defining this "input interleaving" term, it took me a couple reads to understand what they meant).
    This work contributes valuable insights to the field of music AI interface design. It makes strong, substantive arguments in favor of lightweight interactive music AI systems, demonstrating how such systems can contribute meaningfully to musical practices (for example, the authors point out that AI can control many more musical parameters at once than a human can, which facilitates the creation of novel, inhuman-sounding musical artifacts which the authors found useful in a performance context). They argue convincingly for the value of input interleaving, viewing it as a way to steer the AI generation and prevent it from going in undesirable directions. The variety of different systems built from the platform and explored in the case studies shows how the core ideas emobodied by their music AI platform can support different types of music creation (e.g., via synthesizers or DAW). The application of these systems in the case studies shows what it looks like when the systems are actually used in creative performance contexts (and the supplementary performance video sounded good too and made me want to hear more, which is not always the case in music AI research). The authors correctly point out that there has not been enough research into applying lightweight, interactive generative music AI systems to performance practice, and argue convincingly for making generative music systems lightweight, interactive, mapping-focused (rather than training-focused), and integratable into other musical devices. Their input interleaving contribution is a good idea that other people can incorporate into their musical interfaces.

This submission fits "NIME 2026 theme: Communities. Topic 2: ethnographic or sociological study of new interface use outside the lab", in that it describes an autoethnography about using new musical interfaces (i.e. the author's music AI platform and case study implementations) in performance settings. It also fits a number of conference sub-topics, like "ML in musical performance" and "augmented/embedded instruments".

The paper made a few points that shifted my thinking about interactive musical performance. As someone more interested in timbral than symbolic music AI applications, I tend to be skeptical about the value of AI MIDI generation. This work demonstrated to me how AI MIDI generation can actually produce interesting results in a musical performance setting through embedding in existing instruments and co-creation via alternating between AI and human inputs. Prior to reading this paper I already believed strongly in the value of small-data AI and an emphasis on mapping rather than training, but if someone did not already believe in these, the paper could shift their thinking on these too.

This paper would definitely be an interesting read/presentation for NIME conference attendees. It makes substantive arguments in favor of interactive, lightweight, embedded music AI, which it argues persuasively, that are highly relevant to the NIME community.

It demonstrates innovative applications of interactive, embedded music AI and shows how they support musical performance. NIME researchers can use its idea of fast input interleaving, which the paper demonstrates the value of for musical performance contexts. NIME researchers may also want to use the author's AI platform to augment their own MIDI devices (though with the Github page anonymized for review this is harder for me to judge without actually reading their code). I enjoyed the paper, and want to see the authors present it, and I would think other NIME researchers would feel the same.

This paper introduces a generative platform based on Raspberry-pi, running a small data generative model trained on MIDI data to control different configurations of instruments or DAWs.
The paltform is described and applied in the author's artistic practice with four use cases, and an autoethnographic report is provided. The author also introduces a series of notions, such as (re)mapping, and claims of innovative approaches (e.g. fast switchover between humans and AI).

The proposed implementation is interesting and represents in itself a valuable contribution. This makes me lean toward acceptance. However, the paper necessitates work on different crucial areas.

One core contributiion is the notion of (re)mapping, whose definition is rather unclear. How does one practically (re)map a model, and does this term introduces a novel approach or redefines existing ones? If tre)mapping has to do with fast input interleaving with another model (as per the abstract), why is the latter method introduced as something altogether different in 5.3? I suggest to begin by clarifying the notion and its novelty, and showing how this applies in practice through the four examples provided. Additionally, the fast interleaving strategy claimed as a novelty in 5.3 is not new. There exist many conversational instruments adopting similar if not more refined approaches (e.g. see Shepardson's Notochord).

Furthermore, the text is often unclear, sometimes due to missing terms (as in the abstract), others due overly convoluted sentences (e.g. "A learning of the creative process has been that mapping allowed me to find where the generative AI model could have the best musical impact within an intelligent instrument."), and others due to too many ideas expressed in a single sentence.

The argument that "the design space for intelligent musical instruments using present AI techniques has yet to be explored" is weak and unsubstantiated. There are a lot of artists and designers who are exploring this space in the NIME community and beyond. See for instance the interfaces by the Intelligent Instruments Lab, which are not mentioned here. Tuning down such claims does not compromise the novelty of the contribution.

5.3 is a highlight which I suggest to expand, possibly reducing the amount of unnecessary information elsewhere in order for what is relevant to stand out.
